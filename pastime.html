Rock Climbing

Rock climbing is my passion. It's what grounds me when research hits roadblocks or coursework becomes overwhelming. I started climbing in college and now enjoy bouldering, top-roping, and lead climbing across the Northeast. My long-standing nemesis remains "Andrew's Problem" at the Gunks in upstate New York—a humbling reminder that some problems take time.

Since moving to Boston, I've been climbing with the local community at Rumney, Ross Pond, and Lincoln Woods. Through their mentorship, I've learned anchor building, multi-pitch systems, and rope rescue techniques. I am grateful for these learning opportunities and look forward to returning the favor as an instructor someday.

Long Distance Running

Running provides clarity when everything else feels chaotic. To me, running is one of the few things in life where the reward always matches the effort you put in. It reminds me of the value of hard work during my more challenging times.

Over the years, I've run a dozen half-marathons and three marathons (San Francisco, Philadelphia, Burlington). I’m not a fast runner, but I’ve noticed small, steady improvements each time I reach the finish line. My dream is to qualify for and run the Boston Marathon with a sub-2:45 finish, which requires me to be 30% faster than my current best pace.

FPV Drone Racing & Building

FPV (First-Person View) drone racing is a sport where pilots fly small, agile quadcopters at high speeds using live video feed displayed on goggles. It delivers the intensity of Formula 1 racing without putting pilots at risk during high-speed crashes. The same technology has been scaled up by the film industry to capture motor sports and movie chase scenes.

As is common in this hobby, I build my drones from commercially available modular components: carbon fiber frames, brushless motors, flight controls, and analog video transmitters. It's a hands-on mix of electrical engineering, basic aerodynamics, and precision flight control. Unfortunately, crash repair can be a pain since most materials, even carbon fiber, break at such high impact speeds. Apart from amateur racing, I also fly recreationally at hobbyist air fields and in open forest areas.

Photography

When I received the research scholarship from UCLA, I invested part of it in my first personal camera: a Canon R8 with a versatile second-hand zoom lens (RF 24-105mm). Since then, I've captured landscapes in Utah's national parks, wildlife during winter hikes in the White Mountains, portraits at my college graduation, and the vast wilderness of Alaska. I shoot landscapes, wildlife, and portraits—occasionally saving my friends money on professional photographers.

As a researcher in neuroscience, I'm fascinated by how a single photograph can trigger such vivid memory retrieval. Visual stimuli robustly engage hippocampal circuits, facilitating pattern completion and content-addressable memory. These images help me relive not just what I saw, but the full sensory experience of each moment before pressing the shutter—the cold mountain air, the anticipation of the shot, the feeling of being fully present. Those memories are now part of me, and my circuits.
