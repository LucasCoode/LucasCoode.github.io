Nuroscience Research Experience

Harvard Medical School, Research Assistantship

July 2024 - The Present

Themes: System Neuroscience, Computational Neuroscience, Spatial Navigation, Rodent

A way that the brain contributes to the adaptive success of animals through the control and coordination of muscle contractions. Survival of an animal requires the timing of muscle contraction to bear some relation to purpose: “An animal must contract its jaw muscles when its jaw contains something worth biting and contract its leg muscles when there is something worth running to or away from”.

However, as the environment that animals deal with becomes more complex, it very so often happens that the decision for current muscle movements depend on past sensory information no longer present to the animal. Foraging rodents need to remember which patches were already searched to save energy, and humans need to remember where to reach their fingers each morning for their car keys. Both humans and animals demonstrate the ability to use past experiences to plan and simulate future actions, which are important characteristics for higher order brain function and complex decision making.

My work at Lee Lab as a research assistant models these tasks to study memory recall and imagination in rodent model system with spatial navigation paradigm. We train mice and rats to perform spatial navigation tasks that require them to recall past experiences or actively imagine possible future scenarios. We obtain a high temporal resolution read out of the trajectory of their brain states using electrophysiological techniques, which we then use machine learning and statistical methods to interpret. My project in the lab uses a T-maze based delayed nonmatch-to-sample navigation tasks to study how working memory is maintained on a long behavioral time scale.

Cornell University, Undergraduate Research

Sept 2020 - May 2024

Themes: Behavioral Neuroscience, Molecular Biology, Activity Mapping, Danionella dracula

Conducted undergraduate research focusing on activity mapping of Danionella dracula using multiphoton microscopy. This research involved developing protocols for neural activity imaging in small vertebrate models, contributing to our understanding of neural circuit dynamics in transparent organisms.
